üåç Open Source Ecosystem
Why open-weight models matter

Advantages of Open Models:

  • Self-hosting: Run on your own infrastructure. No API costs at scale. Data stays private.
  • Fine-tuning: Customize for specific domains (legal, medical, finance). Closed models don't allow this.
  • No vendor lock-in: Switch providers, modify architecture, no rate limits.
  • Transparency: Inspect weights, understand behavior, audit for safety.

Key Open Model Families:

  • Llama (Meta): Most influential open series. 3.1 405B is flagship. Permissive license for most uses.
  • Mistral (France): European alternative. Strong efficiency focus. Mixtral MoE pioneered open MoE.
  • Qwen (Alibaba): Best Chinese open series. Apache 2.0 license. Excellent multilingual.
  • DeepSeek: Fully open weights. V3 architecture is novel. Research-friendly.

üß† Why AI Progress Enables Robotics
The software-hardware convergence

Key insight: The robotics bottleneck was always the "brain," not the body. Hardware (motors, actuators, sensors) has been ready. What changed is AI can now understand natural language instructions, perceive environments, and adapt to unexpected situations.

What LLMs enable:

  • Natural instruction: "Pick up the box and put it on the shelf" ‚Äî no programming required
  • Common sense: Understanding that fragile items need gentle handling
  • Adaptation: Handling novel situations not explicitly programmed
  • Multi-modal: Vision + language + action in unified model

Remaining challenges:

  • Dexterity: Fine manipulation (threading a needle, tying shoes) still very hard
  • Reliability: Robots must work 24/7 without errors that harm people
  • Cost: Current humanoids cost $50-150K ‚Äî need 10x reduction
  • Battery life: Current runtime 2-4 hours ‚Äî need full workday
  • Edge cases: Handling truly unexpected environments
üíº Economic Implications
What robotics means for labor and cost of living

Jobs most affected (2027-2035):

  • Warehouse/logistics: Already automating. Humanoids accelerate.
  • Manufacturing: Assembly line tasks most vulnerable.
  • Retail: Stocking, inventory, cleaning.
  • Food service: Cooking, prep, delivery (already starting).
  • Agriculture: Harvesting, sorting, packing.
  • Cleaning/janitorial: Commercial buildings first, then residential.

Cost of living impact:

  • Manufactured goods: Could drop significantly as robot labor scales
  • Food: Agricultural + food service automation reduces costs
  • Services: Cleaning, maintenance, delivery get cheaper
  • Healthcare: Robot assistants extend what nurses/aides can do

The big picture: AI alone automates knowledge work (digital). AI + robotics automates physical work too. Combined, they represent potential automation of 60-80% of current jobs by 2040. Whether this is utopia or dystopia depends on policy, transition speed, and how gains are distributed.

Section note: Robotics timelines are highly uncertain. Hardware iteration is slower than software. Regulatory approval, safety certification, and manufacturing scale-up add years. Treat projections as optimistic scenarios.

üìä Executive Summary: Where We Stand
End of 2025 snapshot

As of December 2025, frontier AI models can:

  • Code at ~80% of senior developer level on real-world tasks (SWE-Bench)
  • Reason at 93%+ PhD level on graduate science questions (GPQA)
  • Solve 94% of competition math problems that challenge olympiad contestants (MATH)
  • Work autonomously for ~5 hours before needing human intervention (METR)
  • Process 1-2 million tokens (~750K-1.5M words) in a single context

The cost of frontier AI inference has dropped 97% in 20 months (from $30/M tokens to $1/M). Efficient models now cost $0.10/M tokens‚Äîcheaper than the electricity to run the query.

At current trajectories, by December 2026 we expect: ~94% SWE-Bench (senior+ dev level), ~98% GPQA (near-ceiling), ~78% ARC-AGI (approaching human abstraction), and ~20 hour autonomy (multi-day unsupervised work).

‚ö†Ô∏è Risks & Uncertainties
What could change the trajectory

Technical risks:

  • Scaling limits: Current progress assumes scaling laws continue. They might plateau.
  • Reliability: Models still hallucinate. High-stakes deployment requires near-perfect accuracy.
  • Reasoning depth: ARC-AGI at 45% shows limits in novel abstraction. May not reach human level.

Economic risks:

  • Displacement speed: If automation outpaces job creation, social instability possible.
  • Concentration: AI wealth concentrating in few companies/regions could worsen inequality.
  • Infrastructure costs: Power, chips, data centers require massive ongoing investment.

Geopolitical risks:

  • Chip supply: TSMC in Taiwan fabricates 90%+ of advanced AI chips. Geopolitical risk.
  • Regulation: EU AI Act, potential US regulation could slow deployment.
  • Competition: China's AI development (DeepSeek, Qwen) accelerating despite export controls.

Bottom line: The forecasts in this dashboard assume continued exponential progress. History suggests disruptions, plateaus, and surprises are likely. Treat projections as scenarios, not certainties.

Implications Analysis examines what current AI capabilities and trajectories mean for the economy, workforce, science, and daily life. This analysis assumes current progress rates continue through 2026. Last updated: December 28, 2025. This section will be revised as capabilities evolve.
üìä Executive Summary: Where We Stand
End of 2025 snapshot

As of December 2025, frontier AI models can:

  • Code at ~80% of senior developer level on real-world tasks (SWE-Bench)
  • Reason at 93%+ PhD level on graduate science questions (GPQA)
  • Solve 94% of competition math problems that challenge olympiad contestants (MATH)
  • Work autonomously for ~5 hours before needing human intervention (METR)
  • Process 1-2 million tokens (~750K-1.5M words) in a single context

The cost of frontier AI inference has dropped 97% in 20 months (from $30/M tokens to $1/M). Efficient models now cost $0.10/M tokens‚Äîcheaper than the electricity to run the query.

At current trajectories, by December 2026 we expect: ~94% SWE-Bench (senior+ dev level), ~98% GPQA (near-ceiling), ~78% ARC-AGI (approaching human abstraction), and ~20 hour autonomy (multi-day unsupervised work).

üíº Workforce & Employment
Impact timeline: 2025-2027

Key threshold: When SWE-Bench hits ~85-90%, AI can handle most routine coding tasks end-to-end. Current: 80.9%. Forecast: Q2-Q3 2026.

Already happening (2025):

  • Software teams report 30-50% productivity gains with AI coding assistants
  • Entry-level coding bootcamp graduates face reduced demand‚Äîjunior tasks increasingly automated
  • Customer service, content moderation, and basic copywriting roles declining
  • Legal document review and basic paralegal work being automated

Expected by end of 2026:

  • Software development: Senior developers become "AI managers" overseeing AI-written code. 2-3x output per engineer.
  • Knowledge work: Analysts, researchers, consultants see dramatic productivity gains.
  • Creative work: AI handles first drafts, variations, iterations. Human role shifts to curation and direction.
  • Scientific research: AI accelerates literature review, hypothesis generation, data analysis.

üî¨ Science & Research
The acceleration of discovery

Key threshold: MATH benchmark at 95%+ means AI can assist with novel mathematical proofs. Current: 94.3% (GPT-5.2). We're essentially there.

Current capabilities:

  • AI can review and synthesize scientific literature orders of magnitude faster than humans
  • AlphaFold-style breakthroughs: AI solving protein structures that took decades
  • AI-generated hypotheses being tested in labs (materials science, drug discovery)
  • Mathematical proof assistants finding novel approaches to open problems

2026 expectations:

  • Drug discovery: AI identifies promising compounds 10-100x faster. Clinical trial design optimized. First AI-discovered drugs entering Phase 2/3 trials.
  • Materials science: AI predicts properties of novel materials, accelerating battery, solar, semiconductor research.
  • Mathematics: AI contributes to proofs of significant theorems. Not just verification‚Äîactual creative mathematical reasoning.
  • Climate modeling: Higher-resolution, faster climate simulations. Better prediction of extreme weather.

The big picture: If AI can genuinely accelerate scientific discovery by even 2x, the compounding effects are enormous. Faster drug development, better materials, more efficient energy‚Äîeach breakthrough enables others.

üí∞ Economics & Markets
Capital flows and value creation

Current state:

  • $202B deployed into AI in 2025 (~50% of all VC)
  • $500B OpenAI valuation ‚Äî larger than most countries' GDP
  • $4.65T NVIDIA market cap ‚Äî #1 globally, up 185% YTD
  • $8T+ combined market cap of AI chip makers
  • $280B+ hyperscaler CapEx (AWS, Azure, GCP) ‚Äî mostly AI infrastructure

Where value is accruing:

  • Compute layer: NVIDIA dominates with 80%+ AI GPU market share. AMD, Broadcom gaining.
  • Model layer: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google capturing subscription and API revenue.
  • Application layer: Still nascent‚ÄîCursor, Perplexity, and others building AI-native products.
  • Enterprise: $37B enterprise AI software spend in 2025, growing 40%+ annually.

Macroeconomic implications:

  • Productivity: If AI delivers even 10% economy-wide productivity gain, that's ~$2.5T/year in the US alone.
  • Inflation: AI deflation in digital goods/services could offset inflation elsewhere.
  • Labor share: Risk that productivity gains flow to capital (AI owners) not labor.
  • Concentration: Massive capital requirements favor large players.
üè† Cost of Living & Daily Life
How AI affects household economics

Already cheaper (2025):

  • Tutoring: AI tutors available 24/7 for ~$20/month vs. $50-100/hour human tutors
  • Legal basics: Simple contracts, will drafting, legal research dramatically cheaper
  • Tax prep: AI handles complex returns that previously required accountants
  • Translation: Near-real-time translation essentially free vs. professional rates

Getting cheaper (2026):

  • Software: Custom apps built for specific needs at fraction of current cost
  • Professional services: Basic consulting, analysis, research commoditized
  • Education: Personalized learning at scale
  • Healthcare admin: Appointment scheduling, insurance navigation, basic triage

Still expensive (physical world):

  • Housing, healthcare delivery, childcare, food ‚Äî require physical presence
  • Robotics and physical automation lag software AI significantly

‚ö†Ô∏è Risks & Uncertainties
What could change the trajectory

Technical risks:

  • Scaling limits: Current progress assumes scaling laws continue. They might plateau.
  • Reliability: Models still hallucinate. High-stakes deployment requires near-perfect accuracy.
  • Reasoning depth: ARC-AGI at 45% shows limits in novel abstraction. May not reach human level.

Economic risks:

  • Displacement speed: If automation outpaces job creation, social instability possible.
  • Concentration: AI wealth concentrating in few companies/regions could worsen inequality.
  • Infrastructure costs: Power, chips, data centers require massive ongoing investment.

Geopolitical risks:

  • Chip supply: TSMC in Taiwan fabricates 90%+ of advanced AI chips. Geopolitical risk.
  • Regulation: EU AI Act, potential US regulation could slow deployment.
  • Competition: China's AI development (DeepSeek, Qwen) accelerating despite export controls.

Bottom line: The forecasts in this dashboard assume continued exponential progress. History suggests disruptions, plateaus, and surprises are likely. Treat projections as scenarios, not certainties.
